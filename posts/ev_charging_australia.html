``` "EV Charging Infrastructure in Australia: State of Play and Best Practice"

Executive summary

Australia's public EV charging network is among the weakest in the developed world relative to its EV fleet. At roughly 45 EVs per public charger, Australia trails the IEA benchmark of 10:1 by a factor of five and is 9x worse than the Netherlands. The network is growing at 20% per year from a low base, but deployment patterns --- isolated single chargers, 50 kW legacy hardware, and inconsistent grid connections --- fall short of international best practice.

This report surveys the state of EV charging for passenger cars and trucks separately, covering charger deployment, optimal site design, charging speed, technology, suppliers, and the grid infrastructure that underpins it all. The research draws on IEA, ICCT, and EAFO data, Australian industry reporting, and regulatory frameworks in the EU, US, UK, and China.

Part 1: Passenger Vehicles

1. Current state of public charger deployment

Australia has over 5,000 public EV charging sites. The Electric Vehicle Council counted 1,272 DC fast-charging locations (25 kW+) with 3,436 DC plugs by mid-2025, a 20% increase year-on-year. The remaining sites are AC Level 2 destination chargers (7--22 kW) at shopping centres, hotels, and kerbside locations. By early 2026, DC fast-charge sites had grown to approximately 1,310 locations with over 4,100 plugs [@evinfranews-1272-sites-2025; @cleantechnica-chargers-multiply-2026].

NSW leads with 357 DC locations, followed by Victoria with 311. Coverage thins outside major corridors. Essential Energy launched a "Plug and Play" kerbside charging program in late 2025 to connect 1,000 public chargers to existing power poles in regional and remote NSW [@essential-energy-kerbside-2025].

2. Chargers per EV: Australia vs the world

Australia had approximately 454,000 registered EVs at end-2025 and roughly 5,000 public charging sites (8,000--9,000 plugs). That yields roughly one plug per 50 EVs --- 5x worse than the IEA benchmark of one per 10 [@carsales-charger-ratio-2025; @iea-gevo-2025].

: EVs per Public Charger by Country {#tbl-ratio}

: Source: IEA Global EV Outlook 2025 and 2026 [@iea-gevo-2025; @iea-gevo-2026], EAFO [@eafo-ev-charger-ratio-2025], Statranker [@statranker-chargers-per-ev-2025]

The IEA has shifted emphasis from charger counts to installed public charging capacity per EV, recommending at least 2 kW per electric LDV. The ICCT notes that appropriate ratios depend on home-charging access: markets where most owners charge at home (Norway, USA) can sustain higher vehicle-to-charger ratios than markets with high apartment dwelling (South Korea, Netherlands) where public charging substitutes.

Deloitte estimates Australia needs 27,500 new public chargers by 2033, requiring eight new installations per day [@carsales-charger-ratio-2025].

3. Clusters vs isolated chargers

International practice has shifted toward multi-stall charging hubs. The evidence is consistent across utilisation, economics, reliability, and user experience.

Utilisation. Tesla Supercharger stations average 15.1 ports per site; non-Tesla US DCFC stations average 3.8. Higher port counts reduce the probability that all stalls are occupied. US average DCFC utilisation sits around 12--16% nationally [@stable-auto-utilisation-2025].

Grid connection efficiency. A 6-stall hub shares one high-voltage connection. Dynamic load management distributes power across stalls. The per-stall grid connection cost falls by 30--50% in a hub configuration versus isolated chargers.

Reliability. Single-charger sites have a binary failure mode. Multi-stall hubs degrade gracefully. The US NEVI standard requires 97% average annual uptime per port, which is easier to achieve across a hub [@nevi-standards-2023].

User experience. A hub with 6--12 stalls reduces wait anxiety. Hubs justify co-locating amenities (food, restrooms, retail). The Seed & Greet hub in Hilden, Germany --- 114 charging stations with bakery-cafe and vertical greenhouse --- was named best EV hub in the world in 2024 [@seed-greet-hilden-2024].

International models:

  • US (NEVI): minimum 4 x 150 kW DC fast chargers per site. Total station power at least 600 kW. Sites within one mile of designated EV corridors and no more than 50 miles apart [@nevi-standards-2023].

  • EU (AFIR): DC fast-charging pools every 60 km on TEN-T core network. Minimum 400 kW total site capacity by end-2025 [@afir-regulation-2023].

  • China: highway service areas have 98% charger coverage. Sites routinely have 10--20+ stalls. BYD deploying 4,000 stations with 1 MW chargers [@electrek-china-charging-2025].

Implication for Australia: many Australian sites still have one or two chargers. A minimum 4-stall standard for new public DCFC deployments (similar to NEVI) would improve economics, experience, and reliability. Government-funded sites should mandate minimum stall counts.

4. Charging speed: forward-looking baseline

Australia's early DC network was built around 50 kW chargers. At 50 kW, a 60 kWh battery takes roughly 50--60 minutes for a 10--80% charge. This is inadequate for current vehicles and user expectations.

: Peak DC Charging Acceptance by Vehicle (2025--2026) {#tbl-speeds}

: Source: InsideEVs [@insideevs-fastest-charging-2026], Recurrent Auto (2026)

The modal peak acceptance for new EVs sold in Australia sits around 150--250 kW. 800V architecture vehicles (Ioniq 5/6, EV6/9, Porsche Taycan) sustain high charge rates across a wider state-of-charge window.

Recommended baseline for new deployments (2026--2030):

  • 150 kW minimum for any new public DCFC deployment. This aligns with NEVI's federal minimum and the practical needs of the current vehicle fleet.

  • 350 kW for highway corridor and high-traffic sites. 800V vehicles can use 200--350 kW, and the incremental cost over 150 kW is modest relative to total site cost (grid connection, civil works, land). Charger hardware is a 10--15 year asset; vehicles sold in 2028--2030 will routinely accept 300--400 kW.

Deploying 50 kW chargers in 2026 is indefensible for any publicly funded site.

5. Technology and suppliers (cars)

Charging standards

CCS2 (Combined Charging System Type 2) is the settled DC standard for Australia. Every CCS2-equipped vehicle also accepts Type 2 AC. CHAdeMO is declining; no new models sold in Australia use it. NACS (Tesla's North American standard) is irrelevant to Australia's three-phase grid. Chinese-manufactured vehicles (BYD, MG, GWM, Zeekr) are exported with CCS2 sockets, not GB/T.

Major manufacturers

: Source: Kempower [@kempower-ampol-2025], Exicom [@exicom-tritium-2024], Zecar [@zecar-byd-flash-2026], Evee [@evee-state-of-charge-2026]

Tritium's acquisition by India's Exicom in October 2024 ended the Brisbane company's independent run. The Brisbane R&D centre continues under Exicom ownership [@exicom-tritium-2024].

Technology trends

Liquid-cooled cables. Required above 200 kW. All current-generation 150 kW+ chargers use them. MCS connectors handle over 1,000 A with liquid cooling.

Plug-and-Charge (ISO 15118). Automatic authentication when the cable is connected; no app or RFID card needed. EU AFIR mandates ISO 15118 from 2026. Australian adoption remains limited.

Dynamic power sharing. Kempower's satellite architecture shares a central power unit across up to 8 dispensers. ABB's Terra 360 splits 360 kW across four outlets. Reduces grid connection sizing by 30--50%.

Battery-integrated chargers. BYD's Flash Charging delivers 1,500 kW output via on-site BESS, sidestepping grid constraints. Volvo Energy's PU500 (450--540 kWh battery, 240 kW charger) targets depots [@zecar-byd-flash-2026].

Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G). Four CEC-approved pathways for bidirectional charging in Australia. Wallbox Quasar 2 (11.5 kW bidirectional DC) and RedEarth (22 kW three-phase) are available. BYD is running a warranty-backed V2G trial with Amber Electric.

800V vehicle architectures. Hyundai/Kia E-GMP, Porsche, BYD, Zeekr platforms are transitioning from premium to mass-market default, enabling faster sustained charge rates.

Reliability

A UQ/UWA study found that while Australian networks report 95--98% "uptime" (charger connected to network), the successful-charge rate for drivers is 75--83% [@uq-charger-reliability-2025]. The gap comes from failed handshakes, payment failures, damaged cables, and ICEd bays. Federal-funded chargers must meet a 98% reliability target, but no national mandatory reporting regime exists.

Part 2: Trucks and Heavy Vehicles

6. Current state of electric trucks in Australia

Electric trucks remain nascent in Australia, but the range of available models has expanded since 2024.

Volvo leads with approximately 100 zero-emission trucks on Australian roads. The FH Electric (44t rated, 490 kW, 540 kWh, ~300 km range) and FM Electric are imported now and will be manufactured at Volvo's Wacol plant in Brisbane from late 2027 --- the third global factory to build Volvo electric trucks [@electrek-linfox-2025].

Scania offers the 25P (295 kW motor, up to 300 kWh, ~250 km range). Fuso eCanter second-generation (mid-2025) now offers a 14-model lineup. Mercedes-Benz eActros operates through Centurion's Perth fleet and an Australia Post Melbourne trial.

Tesla Semi confirmed specifications: 822 kWh battery (Long Range), 800 km range, 1.2 MW Megacharger capability. US deliveries commenced in 2026; no Australian date announced [@electrek-tesla-semi-specs-2026].

7. Truck charging requirements vs cars

Battery sizes differ by an order of magnitude:

: Battery Capacity and Range by Vehicle Type {#tbl-battery-sizes}

: Source: Scania Group (2025), Electrek [@electrek-tesla-semi-specs-2026], manufacturer specifications

Charging time constraints:

  • At 350 kW (current CCS2 maximum): a 520 kWh battery charges 20--80% in approximately 65 minutes --- too slow for a single rest break.

  • At 1.2 MW (MCS): the same battery recovers ~60% in approximately 30 minutes, fitting within a rest break.

  • At depot overnight (50--150 kW): 4--10 hours, fitting within an 8-hour depot window using lower-cost hardware.

About 70% of heavy-duty trucks drive less than 500 km per day. For these duty cycles, depot charging overnight at 50--150 kW is sufficient and cost-optimal. En-route high-power charging is needed for long-haul operations exceeding single-charge range and multi-shift operations.

8. Megawatt Charging System (MCS)

The MCS standard has reached commercial maturity in 2026:

  • IEC 63379 (connector and pin assignment): v1.0 published February 2026

  • SAE J3271 (North American variant): published March 2025

  • Maximum power: 3.75 MW (1,250 V DC at 3,000 A); initial commercial implementations at 1.0--1.2 MW

Kempower deployed the first public MCS charging session in Sweden (August 2025) and California (March 2026). The new Mega Satellite Flex supports both CCS (up to 560 kW) and MCS (up to 1.2 MW) from a single unit, on sale from July 2026 [@electrek-kempower-mega-2026; @charin-mcs-2026].

ABB demonstrated megawatt charging on a MAN eTruck. Tesla's proprietary Megacharger (1.2 MW) network has 66 US locations announced. Delta Electronics confirmed 1 MW hardware for Australia within 12 months.

Interim solution: CCS2 at 150--400 kW. This is what NewVolt (Australia) and most European truck charging networks deploy. Adequate for metro distribution but marginal for long-haul.

9. Charging models for trucks

International experience points to a three-tier model:

Tier 1 --- Depot charging (overnight, 50--150 kW). The primary model for most fleets. Trucks charge at lower-cost hardware during 8-hour windows. Centurion's Perth depot demonstrates the extreme case: 30 trucks charged entirely by 4.4 MW solar + 10.3 MWh BESS, fully off-grid [@thedriven-centurion-2025].

Tier 2 --- Logistics precinct hubs (400 kW--1.2 MW). NewVolt is building Australia's first shared-use truck charging network: 14 locations in Melbourne, Sydney, and Brisbane with 400 kW per bay (dual CCS2). ARENA committed $25.3 million in co-funding. First bays operational late 2026 [@electrive-newvolt-2026].

Tier 3 --- Highway corridor hubs (1--3 MW per site). The EU mandates truck charging every 60 km on TEN-T core network with minimum 350 kW per connector and 1,400 kW minimum site capacity [@afir-regulation-2023]. Australia has no equivalent corridor mandate. The Hume Highway (Sydney--Melbourne, 880 km, ~4,800 trucks/day) is the first corridor targeted by NewVolt. New Energy Transport is developing a Wilton site (10 x 400 kW) [@thedriven-net-wilton-2026].

Clusters vs isolated (trucks)

The case for clusters is stronger for trucks than for cars:

  • Grid connection costs dominate truck charging economics; sharing a single MV connection across 10--20 bays is cost-effective.

  • Truck operators need predictable availability; a multi-bay hub with 24/7 operation reduces scheduling risk.

  • Co-locating with logistics precincts captures fleet density --- most truck kilometres originate from a small number of freight nodes.

10. Technology and suppliers (trucks)

Kempower leads in distributed depot architecture. The satellite system (central power unit + up to 8 dispensers) suits truck depots. The Mega Satellite Flex bridges the CCS-to-MCS transition [@kempower-mcs-2026].

ABB supplies Terra HP (175--350 kW) and Pantograph Down systems for bus depots. MCS prototype demonstrated.

Siemens (Heliox) leads in pantograph-based bus and truck charging (acquired Heliox 2023).

Tritium/Exicom continues the Tritium-designed range under new ownership.

Pantograph charging automates the connection --- relevant for depots where drivers should not handle heavy MCS cables. Governed by the OppCharge industry interface; in pilot phase for trucks.

Depot management software allocates power across vehicles by departure time, SOC, and tariff window. Key platforms: ChargePilot (The Mobility House), Synop, Ampcontrol, ChargePoint Fleet.

11. Australian policy gaps for trucks

Australia lacks three policy instruments driving truck electrification internationally:

  1. No heavy vehicle CO~2~ standard. The EU mandates 45% fleet-average CO~2~ reduction for new trucks by 2030 and 90% by 2040. Australia's regulations remain at Euro V equivalent.

  2. No corridor charging mandate. The EU requires truck charging every 60--100 km on major corridors. Australia relies on voluntary commercial investment.

  3. No purchase incentives for heavy vehicles. No federal zero-emission truck subsidy equivalent to the US IRA's $40,000 commercial clean vehicle credit. NSW expanded its fleet incentive to include some truck categories [@nsw-ev-strategy-2026].

Part 3: Grid Infrastructure

12. Power requirements by site type

: Source: Kempower, industry estimates

A 4-stall 350 kW site at 1.6 MW exceeds the capacity of any standard suburban distribution transformer (200--500 kVA). A truck depot with 10--20 MCS chargers needs 10--20 MW --- equivalent to a small industrial substation.

13. Grid connection: the binding constraint

Grid connections are the largest barrier to charging network expansion. In April 2026, fifteen organisations --- including Tesla, AGL, EnergyAustralia, the Clean Energy Council, and Jolt --- issued a joint statement calling out slow, costly, and unpredictable connections as the primary bottleneck. The EVC notes that DNSP monopolies control approximately 90% of the costs facing the EV charging market [@thedriven-grid-barriers-2026].

Connection timeframes: 6--24 months for sites above 500 kW. For sites requiring substation augmentation, lead times stretch beyond two years.

Metro vs regional: In metropolitan areas, existing feeders often have spare capacity for sub-500 kW sites ($50,000--200,000, 3--6 months). In regional areas, feeders are long, lightly loaded, and often single-wire earth return (SWER). A 150 kW charger at a regional highway stop may require 5--20 km of feeder reinforcement.

Tariff disparities: At a 500 kVA site consuming 100 MWh/year, annual network charges range from $9,000 (Endeavour Energy) to $70,000 (Ausgrid) --- a 7:1 ratio [@evc-ausgrid-submission-2022].

14. Solutions to grid constraints

Battery-buffered charging

A BESS charges from the grid slowly and discharges to EVs at peak rates. A site can operate 1.4 MW of chargers on a 200 kW grid connection. Kempower has partnered with Polarium to integrate LFP BESS with DC fast chargers [@kempower-polarium-bess-2025]. The US DOE documents deployments where 200 kW grid supports 600 kW+ charger capacity [@nrel-battery-buffered-2024].

Cost trade-off: a 1 MWh BESS adds $500,000--800,000 to site capital but may avoid $1--2 million in grid augmentation and reduce demand charges by 40--70%.

BYD's Flash Charging system takes this further: 1,500 kW output via on-site BESS, entirely decoupling charger power from grid connection capacity [@zecar-byd-flash-2026].

On-site solar + storage

A 100 kW rooftop array generates 400--500 kWh/day in Australian conditions, offsetting 15--25% of a busy 6-stall site's daily throughput. Combined solar-plus-storage can reduce peak demand charges by 40--70%. Centurion's Perth truck depot demonstrates full off-grid viability: 4.4 MW solar + 10.3 MWh BESS for 30 trucks [@thedriven-centurion-2025].

Smart charging / load management

For depots with 10+ chargers, load management distributes available power across plugs, prioritises vehicles by departure time, and shifts load to off-peak tariffs. A 50-vehicle fleet needing 2 MW of aggregate energy can operate on a 500 kW connection by staggering overnight charging across an 8-hour window, cutting demand charges by up to 75%.

Grid augmentation: who pays?

Under current AER rules, the connecting customer pays for dedicated assets and contributes to shared network augmentation where the cost-revenue test shows a shortfall [@aer-connection-guidelines-2023]. Industry is calling for "make ready" obligations where DNSPs proactively build capacity to anticipated charging locations and socialise costs across the tariff base.

Three competing AEMC rule changes are in progress: Energy Networks Australia proposes DNSP-led charging infrastructure as a regulated service; Nexa Advisory proposes reinforcing ring-fencing to keep DNSPs out of the competitive market; DCCEEW proposes amendments to facilitate Commonwealth-funded rollout [@aemc-dnsp-ev-charging-2026].

Part 4: Best Practice and Policy

16. International frameworks Australia should learn from

EU --- AFIR

The most prescriptive framework globally. DC fast-charging pools every 60 km on motorways; minimum 150 kW per connector; contactless payment mandatory; truck charging every 60 km on TEN-T core. Australia's 150 km highway target interval is less ambitious [@afir-regulation-2023].

US --- NEVI

$5 billion program. Minimum 4 x 150 kW per site, 97% annual uptime per port with defined measurement methodology, OCPP 2.0.1 mandatory [@nevi-standards-2023]. The 97% uptime requirement with enforcement teeth is the standout. Australia's Minimum Operating Standards lack equivalent penalties [@dcceew-min-operating-standards-2024].

UK

Ofgem's ED3 framework allows anticipatory investment in network capacity ahead of confirmed EV demand. The companion "build and flex" strategy requires maximising existing capacity through smart charging and BESS before building new assets [@ofgem-ed3-2026].

California

The benchmark for utility-led infrastructure. Assembly Bill 841 directs utilities to install electrical infrastructure to the charger pad at no cost ("utility-side make-ready"). A $1 billion transportation electrification program: 70% for medium- and heavy-duty, 30% for light-duty at apartments [@cpuc-te-programs-2025].

Germany --- Deutschlandnetz

Rather than subsidising individual operators, the government tendered 1,100 locations for HPC infrastructure with binding construction obligations --- a shift from subsidy to tendered deployment [@nationale-leitstelle-deutschlandnetz-2025].

China

Treats charging as industrial policy. The 2025 Three-Year Action Plan targets 28 million chargers and 300 GW of public capacity by 2027. State-owned grid corporations build dedicated feeders and substations for large hubs, socialising costs across the customer base [@peoples-daily-china-charging-2025; @electrek-china-charging-2025].

Building code mandates

The EU EPBD IV requires one charger per 2 parking spaces in new office buildings and pre-cabling of 50% of spaces in existing large car parks. California's CalGreen Code requires 100% EV-ready parking in new multifamily dwellings. Australia has no national building code requirement for EV charging in new buildings. This is a gap.

18. Summary of gaps and recommendations

  1. Charger-to-EV ratio. Australia's ~45:1 ratio is 5x worse than the IEA benchmark. Meeting 10:1 requires sustained deployment of 8+ chargers per day through 2033.

  2. Mandate clusters. Government-funded sites should require a minimum of 4 DC fast-charging stalls (matching US NEVI). Isolated single-charger deployments waste capital and deliver poor user experience.

  3. Set a speed floor. 150 kW minimum for any new publicly funded DC charger. 350 kW for highway and high-traffic sites. Ban 50 kW deployments from public funding.

  4. Fix grid connections. Standardise DNSP connection processes and timelines (target: 3 months for sub-1 MW, 6 months for 1--5 MW). Adopt "make ready" obligations or anticipatory investment frameworks.

  5. Battery-buffered by default. For grid-constrained sites, BESS-integrated charging (Kempower/Polarium, BYD Flash Charging) should be the default architecture, not a workaround.

  6. Building code mandates. Require EV-ready parking in all new residential and commercial construction, following EU EPBD and California CalGreen precedent.

  7. Truck-specific policy. Legislate heavy vehicle CO~2~ standards. Mandate corridor charging on major freight routes. Establish a federal zero-emission truck purchase incentive.

  8. Enforce uptime. Adopt a measurable uptime standard (97% per port, monthly measurement) with penalties for persistent non-compliance, following the NEVI model.

  9. Payment interoperability. Mandate contactless card payment at all public DC chargers (no app required), consistent with EU AFIR.
