# The Politics of US Data Centres: Bills, Equity and the Backlash Going Mainstream

**Research draft — May 2026**
**Scope**: political, policy and generalised dimensions of opposition to US data centres. The local-opposition draft covers individual zoning fights; this draft focuses on the rate-making, environmental-justice, federal-regulatory and electoral overlay.

---

## Introduction

Two years ago opposition to data centres was a story about traffic, noise and the loss of farmland in particular counties. By mid-2026 it has become a national political story driven by three converging shocks: the PJM capacity auctions of July 2025 and December 2025 — both of which cleared at administrative caps and have been attributed by the PJM market monitor to data centre demand; a wave of state legislation in 2025 and early 2026 (more than 300 bills in 2025 alone) seeking to make hyperscalers pay their own grid costs; and a sequence of FERC orders culminating in the December 2025 PJM co-location order that have re-opened the question of whether large loads can take service from generators without paying transmission charges.

The political colouration of opposition is unusual. Republican legislators in Texas, Georgia and Ohio have advanced cost-allocation rules at least as aggressive as those proposed by Democrats in Virginia, New Jersey and Maryland. The Trump administration is the most pro-data-centre actor in the system — Stargate was announced from the White House on Trump's second day in office — while state regulators across the partisan spectrum are pushing back. Polls now show 35 per cent of Virginia voters comfortable with new data centres in their community, down from 69 per cent in 2023.

This draft pulls together the rate-impact evidence, the environmental-justice cases, the partisan dynamics, the federal/state regulatory shifts, the industry response, and the polling. It closes with a short evidence-based judgement on whether attitudes are changing.

## 1. Residential bill impacts as a political issue

### PJM capacity prices: the central data point

The PJM Interconnection 2025/26 Base Residual Auction, held in July 2024, cleared at $269.92/MW-day across most of the RTO — an 833 per cent jump from $28.92/MW-day in the prior auction. The Baltimore Gas and Electric zone cleared at $466.35/MW-day and the Dominion zone at $444.26/MW-day [Source: https://insidelines.pjm.com/pjm-auction-procures-134479-mw-of-generation-resources/; https://www.utilitydive.com/news/pjm-interconnection-capacity-auction-data-center/808264/]. The 2026/27 BRA, held in July 2025, cleared at the new $329.17/MW-day administrative price cap across the entire RTO, MAAC and EMAAC zones, with cleared volumes only 139 MW above the reliability requirement [Source: https://www.pjm.com/-/media/DotCom/markets-ops/rpm/rpm-auction-info/2026-2027/2026-2027-bra-report.pdf; https://www.enelnorthamerica.com/insights/blogs/pjm-2026-2027-capacity-auction-results].

The Independent Market Monitor for PJM (Monitoring Analytics) attributed the 2025/26 increase primarily to data centre demand. In a November 2025 filing, Monitoring Analytics estimated that data centres accounted for 63 per cent of the price increase in the 2025/26 auction, equivalent to $9.3 billion of the $14.7 billion total capacity bill [Source: https://www.monitoringanalytics.com/filings/2025/IMM_Complaint_re_Data_Center_Loads_Docket_No_EL26-XX_20251125.pdf; https://www.utilitydive.com/news/data-centers-pjm-capacity-auction-market-monitor/801780/]. Looking across the most recent three auctions, Monitoring Analytics calculated that data centre forecasts contributed 45 per cent of $47.2 billion in cumulative capacity costs [Source: https://www.utilitydive.com/news/data-centers-pjm-capacity-auction/808951/]. IEEFA characterised the same situation as data centres spurring PJM capacity prices "by a factor of 10" [Source: https://ieefa.org/resources/projected-data-center-growth-spurs-pjm-capacity-prices-factor-10].

### Translation to residential bills

IEEFA and consumer-advocate analyses translate the wholesale numbers as follows:

- **Pepco (DC)**: average residential bills rose by about $21/month from June 2025, with roughly half attributable to the capacity-price spike [Source: https://introl.com/blog/pjm-rate-shock-100-billion-data-center-electricity-2026].
- **Western Maryland**: about $18/month average increase from PJM capacity prices [Source: https://ieefa.org/resources/projected-data-center-growth-spurs-pjm-capacity-prices-factor-10].
- **Ohio (general)**: about $16/month; **Columbus, Ohio specifically**: about $27/month [Source: https://introl.com/blog/pjm-rate-shock-100-billion-data-center-electricity-2026].
- **Aggregate**: from June 2026 ratepayers across PJM will pay an additional $1.4 billion per year in capacity-market costs versus the prior year, on top of the 2025/26 increase [Source: https://ieefa.org/resources/projected-data-center-growth-spurs-pjm-capacity-prices-factor-10].

NRDC has projected $163 billion in cumulative PJM capacity-market costs through 2033 if data centre forecasts are realised [Source: https://www.citizensutilityboard.org/blog/2025/10/23/data-center-concerns-cub-nrdc-experts-warn-pjm-states-could-get-hit-with-forced-blackouts-163b-in-additional-electricity-capacity-costs/]. (NRDC is an advocacy organisation and that figure is contingent on the load forecast being realised.)

### Caveats on attribution

PJM capacity prices reflect retiring coal, lagging interconnection-queue throughput and rising gas prices in addition to data centre demand. The figures above use Monitoring Analytics' methodology, which attributes a marginal share of the price increase to incremental data centre load. JLARC's December 2024 study reached the more nuanced conclusion that historical cost-shifts from data centres to other Virginia ratepayers had been "minimal" because rate design correctly assigned incurred costs, but warned that going forward a large amount of new generation and transmission would have to be built that "would not otherwise be built", creating fixed costs that all customers ultimately recover. JLARC projected Dominion residential bills could rise by $444 per year by 2040 due to data centre growth [Source: https://jlarc.virginia.gov/pdfs/reports/Rpt598-2.pdf; https://www.pwcva.gov/assets/2024-12/JLARC%20Report%20Summary_12-16-2024.pdf].

### State rate cases

**Dominion Virginia (2025 biennial review)**: The Virginia State Corporation Commission in November 2025 approved a $565.7 million base-rate increase for 2026 ($209.9 million more for 2027), well below Dominion's request of $822 million. Average residential bills rise by $11.24/month in January 2026 and a further $2.36 in January 2027. The SCC also approved a new GS-5 rate class for customers of 25 MW or more (effective January 2027), under which large-load customers must pay for at least 85 per cent of contracted distribution and transmission demand and 60 per cent of generation demand. Cost-allocation methodology will be revisited in the next rate case [Source: https://virginiamercury.com/2025/11/25/scc-approves-chesterfield-gas-plant-and-dominion-rate-hike-creates-new-rate-class-for-data-centers/; https://www.scc.virginia.gov/about-the-scc/newsreleases/release/scc-issues-order-on-dev-biennial-review-2025/scc-rules-in-dev-biennial-review-case.html; https://www.loudounnow.com/news/scc-approves-new-data-center-rate-class-for-dominion/article_02d5fa0c-1ed8-4771-b57d-c24765739854.html].

**Georgia Power (2025 IRP)**: The Georgia Public Service Commission unanimously approved a stipulated agreement in December 2025 allowing Georgia Power to add 9,885 MW of new capacity, the bulk of which serves new large customers including data centres. The package includes five new gas units at Plant Bowen, Plant McIntosh and Plant Wansley. The settlement promises "downward pressure" of at least $8.50/month on residential rates, although environmental groups including Sierra Club (an advocacy organisation) filed a motion seeking to delay the decision on the basis that Georgia Power had not produced the underlying math for that promise [Source: https://georgiarecorder.com/2025/12/19/georgia-regulators-approve-massive-power-grid-expansion-to-serve-data-centers/; https://www.sierraclub.org/press-releases/2025/12/georgia-public-service-commission-issues-final-order-data-center-power-plan]. Base rates are frozen for three years from July 2025 [Source: https://psc.ga.gov/site/downloads/datacenterfactsheet.pdf].

**Entergy Louisiana (Meta Hyperion)**: The Louisiana PSC voted 4-1 in August 2025 to approve three new gas plants for Meta's Hyperion AI campus in Richland Parish. In April 2026 the PSC voted 4-1 again to fast-track applications for seven additional gas plants for a second Meta site (Project Everest), bringing the total to ten gas plants for Meta in Louisiana [Source: https://lailluminator.com/2025/08/20/entergy-meta/; https://fortune.com/2026/03/27/meta-hyperion-10-gas-power-plants-louisiana-entergy/; https://www.wbrz.com/news/psc-votes-4-1-to-fast-track-entergy-s-proposal-for-billions-in-energy-investments-to-power-meta-data-center]. Commissioner Davante Lewis was the sole dissent in both votes. He argued that Meta is contractually committed for only 15-20 years — about half the lifespan of a gas turbine — leaving Louisiana ratepayers with potential stranded-cost exposure he placed at "about a 25 per cent risk" [Source: https://www.krvs.org/louisiana-news/2026-04-14/entergy-wants-to-fast-track-21b-meta-deal-as-psc-commissioner-advocates-sound-alarm; https://thelensnola.org/2026/04/14/meta-project-evest-louisiana-energy-expansion-costs-entergy/].

**AEP Ohio (data centre tariff)**: The Public Utilities Commission of Ohio approved AEP Ohio's data centre tariff settlement on 9 July 2025; it took effect on 23 July 2025. The tariff requires large new data centre customers (load greater than 25 MW) to pay for a minimum of 85 per cent of subscribed electricity for up to 12 years (a 4-year ramp followed by an 8-year minimum). AEP Ohio's data centre load grew from roughly 100 MW in 2020 to 600 MW in 2024, with a forecast 5 GW by 2030 and more than 30,000 MW in the queue. Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft and the Ohio Manufacturers' Association challenged the tariff; PUCO denied the appeal in November 2025; the OMA has signalled possible Ohio Supreme Court appeal [Source: https://www.aepohio.com/company/about/rates/data-center-tariff/; https://kjk.com/2025/11/14/regulating-surge-legal-analysis-aep-ohios-data-center-tariff-and-pucos-approval/; https://www.datacenterfrontier.com/energy/article/55304787/ohio-sets-new-precedent-aeps-power-rules-shift-data-center-cost-burden].

**PPL Electric (Pennsylvania)**: A 2026 settlement subject to PUC approval would create a new large-load rate class for data centres above 50 MW peak (or aggregations of 75 MW within 10 miles), requires minimum 10-year service agreements with stranded-cost protection, and provides $11 million for low-income rate relief. Residential rates rise by 4.9 per cent on 1 July 2026 (about $7.42/month for a 1,000 kWh user, plus a $15 monthly customer charge) [Source: https://www.utilitydive.com/news/ppl-electric-rate-case-settlement-data-center-tariff/814760/; https://whyy.org/articles/pennsylvania-electricity-costs-data-centers/; https://radio.wpsu.org/2026-03-20/precedent-setting-pa-rate-case-would-protect-residential-electricity-customers-from-data-center-costs].

### State legislation

**Virginia (2026 session)**: More than 60 data centre-related bills were filed. The two flagship measures were SB 253 (Sen. Lucas) and HB 1393, which would direct the SCC to allocate the cost of new generating capacity to customers with 25 MW or more of demand. The SCC estimated SB 253 would cut typical residential rates by 3.4 per cent (about $5.52/month) while raising data centre rates by 15.8 per cent. Governor Spanberger amended the bills on 16 April 2026, removing the explicit cost-shift mechanism and replacing it with softer language directing the SCC to ensure no cross-subsidisation; she also added a 9.3 per cent return-on-equity cap with refunds for over-earnings. The General Assembly rejected most of the amendments on 22 April 2026 and sent the original bills back. The data centre tax-break debate held up the state budget [Source: https://virginiamercury.com/2026/04/16/lawmakers-dominion-say-spanbergers-amendments-weaken-bill-to-shift-costs-onto-data-centers/; https://virginiamercury.com/2026/04/23/va-lawmakers-ok-governors-tweaks-to-major-energy-bills-reject-health-and-labor-bill-amendments/; https://introl.com/blog/virginia-sb-253-data-center-electricity-rate-shift-2026].

**Texas SB 6**: Signed into law in late June 2025, applies to customers above 75 MW (threshold adjustable by the PUC). Imposes site-control documentation, financial commitment and a $100,000 minimum fee for transmission-screening applications — explicitly framed in the legislation as a defence against "phantom load" inflating ERCOT planning. ERCOT gains narrow authority to remotely curtail large loads during grid emergencies; behind-the-meter generation associated with new large loads can also be curtailed in defined circumstances [Source: https://www.bakerbotts.com/thought-leadership/publications/2025/july/texas-senate-bill-6-understanding-the-impacts-to-large-loads-and-co-located-generation; https://www.utilitydive.com/news/texas-law-gives-grid-operator-power-to-disconnect-data-centers-during-crisi/751587/; https://www.mcguirewoods.com/client-resources/alerts/2025/7/texas-senate-bill-6-significantly-expands-regulatory-oversight-over-large-loads-in-ercot/]. The original brief refers to SB 2627; this companion bill does not appear in standard legal summaries of the Texas data centre legislation and may be misnumbered.

**Oregon POWER Act**: Passed June 2025 with bipartisan support — first state to create a dedicated data centre electricity rate class [Source: https://www.utilitydive.com/news/ferc-pjm-colocation-data-center/808368/].

**Maryland**: Prince George's County imposed a 180-day data centre permit moratorium in September 2025. State legislation under consideration in 2026 would require new data centres to be paired with new gas or nuclear (including SMR) generation [Source: https://conduitstreet.mdcounties.org/2025/09/24/prince-georges-pauses-data-center-approvals-amid-policy-review/; https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/maryland-citizens-slapped-with-usd2-billion-grid-upgrade-bill-for-out-of-state-ai-data-centers-state-complains-to-federal-energy-regulators-says-additional-cost-breaks-ratepayer-protection-pledge-promises].

**Aggregate**: Legislators across both parties introduced more than 238 data centre bills in all 50 states in 2025; over 40 were enacted in 21 states. The three dominant categories are rate-structure reform, tax-incentive rollbacks, and construction moratoriums [Source: https://www.powerpolicy.net/p/data-centers-could-make-or-break]. MultiState tracked over 300 bills across 2025, rising further in 2026 [Source: https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/2/20/state-data-center-legislation-in-2026-tackles-energy-and-tax-issues].

### Tax-break rollbacks

Virginia's data centre sales-tax exemption cost the state $1.6 billion in foregone revenue in FY2025, up 118 per cent from FY2024. When created in 2008 the exemption had been projected to cost $1.54 million per year — the actual cost now exceeds the original estimate by more than 100,000 per cent. The Virginia Senate has proposed accelerating the exemption sunset from 2035 to January 2027; the House version preserves it through 2035 with new clean-energy conditions. The dispute was holding up budget negotiations as of late April 2026 [Source: https://goodjobsfirst.org/virginia-tax-revenue-losses-to-data-centers-soar-to-1-6-billion-for-fy25/; https://www.theregister.com/2026/01/07/datacenter_tax_breaks_virginia; https://virginiamercury.com/2026/04/27/data-center-tax-exemption-changes-still-holding-up-virginia-budget/; https://rga.lis.virginia.gov/Published/2026/RD40/PDF].

## 2. Environmental justice

### Memphis xAI Colossus

xAI began operating its Colossus data centre in South Memphis in June 2024. To power it, Elon Musk's company installed approximately 35 methane gas turbines without an air-quality permit. The Shelby County Health Department issued an air permit on 2 July 2025 covering 15 of the turbines [Source: https://www.actionnews5.com/2025/07/02/health-dept-grants-permit-xai-turbines/; https://www.fox13memphis.com/health/shelby-county-permits-15-turbines-at-xai-supercomputer-environmentalists-to-conduct-air-testing/article_01244e83-03fc-41f3-979c-abc3a944cf1b.html]. The NAACP, represented by the Southern Environmental Law Center and Earthjustice, appealed the permits in July 2025. The appeal hearing before the Memphis and Shelby County Air Pollution Control Board was scheduled for 15 December 2025 [Source: https://tennesseelookout.com/briefs/naacp-others-appeal-xai-turbine-permits-for-memphis-data-center/].

In April 2026 the NAACP filed a federal Clean Air Act lawsuit against xAI and its subsidiary MZX Tech alleging illegal operation of 27 unpermitted gas turbines for a Colossus 2 facility located across the state line in Southaven, Mississippi. SELC and Earthjustice have characterised the Southaven plant as potentially the largest industrial NOx source in the greater Memphis area, with capacity to emit more than 1,700 tons of NOx, 180 tons of particulate matter, 500 tons of carbon monoxide and 19 tons of formaldehyde per year [Source: https://earthjustice.org/case/xai-illegal-gas-power-plant-data-center-colossus; https://earthjustice.org/press/2026/xai-sued-for-illegal-power-plant; https://naacp.org/articles/naacp-sues-xai-illegal-pollution-data-center-power-plant; https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/14/elon-musk-xai-memphis-data-centers.html; https://www.selc.org/news/xai-built-an-illegal-power-plant-to-power-its-data-center/]. (SELC, Earthjustice and the NAACP are all advocacy/litigation organisations.)

The environmental-justice frame is built on three facts: South Memphis is majority-Black; Memphis was named an "asthma capital" and Shelby County and DeSoto County both received an "F" for ozone from the American Lung Association; and tens of thousands of people live within close range of the turbines [Source: https://earthjustice.org/article/a-community-takes-a-data-center-colossus-to-court]. The Shelby County permit decision has itself become a political flashpoint, with local groups calling on the Health Department to rescind permits in December 2025 [Source: https://www.actionnews5.com/2025/12/09/local-orgs-call-shelby-county-health-department-rescind-permits-colossus-facility/].

### Other equity-flagged cases

The political Memphis story is also the most legally advanced equity case, but several others have surfaced:
- **Louisiana**: the Meta Hyperion gas-plant build-out is in Richland Parish, an area with relatively low household incomes. Commissioner Davante Lewis has framed his dissent partly in environmental-justice terms in addition to ratepayer-protection terms [Source: https://thelensnola.org/2026/04/14/meta-project-evest-louisiana-energy-expansion-costs-entergy/].
- **Prince William County, Virginia (Digital Gateway)**: opposition has fused historic-site protection (Manassas battlefield) with concerns about disproportionate siting of high-impact infrastructure in lower-income parts of the county. Plans for the project were abandoned in early 2026 [Source: https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2026/04/15/data-centers-poll-virginia/].

### Federal Title VI complaints

The available reporting does not surface any specific Title VI complaint to the EPA targeting a data centre as of mid-2026 — the xAI litigation is being pursued under the Clean Air Act citizen-suit provisions rather than through Title VI of the Civil Rights Act. (See https://www.epa.gov/ogc/title-vi-and-environmental-justice-epa for the general Title VI framework.) The xAI case may evolve into a Title VI matter but as of April 2026 the live federal action is the Clean Air Act suit.

### Academic literature on equity

The Stanford Law Review's May 2025 paper on Title VI environmental-justice claims [Source: https://law.stanford.edu/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Sivas-FINAL.pdf] and the Center for Public Integrity's reporting on EPA's historical failure to enforce Title VI [Source: https://publicintegrity.org/environment/pollution/environmental-justice-denied/environmental-discrimination-epa-complaint-title-vi-civil-rights/] provide context but do not contain large-scale empirical analysis of data centre siting equity. There is a clear gap in the published academic literature on this specific question.

## 3. Partisan and ideological dynamics

### The unusual coalition

Data Center Watch — an industry-funded tracker — found that of identifiable elected-official opposition between 2023 and Q2 2025, Republicans accounted for 55 per cent and Democrats 45 per cent. Republican objections more often centred on tax incentives and grid reliability; Democratic objections more often centred on environmental impact and equity. The 2025 cycle saw $64 billion in projects blocked or delayed, rising to $98 billion by Q2 2025 [Source: https://www.datacenterwatch.org/report; https://www.datacenterwatch.org/q22025; https://virginiamercury.com/2025/05/21/report-highlights-community-pushback-stalling-64-billion-in-data-center-development-nationwide/].

NPR characterised the politics as "rare bipartisan agreement" on regulating AI and data centres at the state level, with the issue cutting across the usual lines [Source: https://www.npr.org/2026/02/26/nx-s1-5726431/data-centers-ai-trump-housing-states; https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/politics-news/reining-data-centers-sparks-rare-bipartisanship-statehouses-rcna262990]. CNBC reported in April 2026 that Pennsylvania GOP incumbents were facing real electoral threats over the issue [Source: https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/24/ai-data-centers-pennsylvania-republicans-2026-election.html]. Local public radio in Texas headlined "Texas Republicans Have a Data Center Problem" in May 2026 [Source: https://www.tpr.org/news/2026-05-07/texas-republicans-have-a-data-center-problem].

The intra-party splits are equally interesting: in Oregon, Democratic Governor Tina Kotek proposed expanding data centre tax breaks while Democratic legislators passed a temporary tax-break suspension; in Georgia, Republicans have split on whether to constrain data-centre development [Source: https://www.opb.org/article/2026/02/26/on-ai-and-data-centers-state-lawmakers-find-bipartisan-agreement/].

### Trump administration position

The Trump administration is the most consistent pro-data-centre actor in US politics. The Stargate joint venture (OpenAI, Oracle, SoftBank, MGX) was unveiled at the White House on 21 January 2025, with an announced $100 billion in immediate commitments and up to $500 billion over four years [Source: https://www.cnn.com/2025/01/21/tech/openai-oracle-softbank-trump-ai-investment; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stargate_LLC; https://time.com/7209167/stargate-openai-donald-trump/]. On 23 July 2025 the President issued Executive Order 14148 ("Accelerating Federal Permitting of Data Center Infrastructure") covering AI-dedicated facilities of more than 100 MW, alongside the "Winning the Race: America's AI Action Plan" [Source: https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/07/accelerating-federal-permitting-of-data-center-infrastructure/; https://www.hunton.com/the-nickel-report/president-trump-announces-ai-action-plan-and-accelerated-permitting-and-financing-for-datacenters].

On 23 May 2025 Trump signed four nuclear-energy executive orders directing reform of the NRC and tasking DOE with siting and approving advanced reactors to power data centres, designating AI data centres as critical defence facilities [Source: https://www.energy.gov/ne/articles/9-key-takeaways-president-trumps-executive-orders-nuclear-energy]. On 24 July 2025 DOE selected four federal sites (Idaho National Laboratory, Oak Ridge, Paducah, Savannah River) to solicit data centre development [Source: https://www.pillsburylaw.com/en/news-and-insights/AI-data-center-DOE-site-trump.html].

In March 2026 the White House released a "Ratepayer Protection Pledge" aimed at PJM-state governors [Source: https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/03/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-advances-energy-affordability-with-the-ratepayer-protection-pledge/]. It is at least nominally responsive to the rate backlash, although Maryland subsequently complained to FERC that additional grid-cost allocations to its ratepayers for out-of-state data centres breach those pledges [Source: https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/maryland-citizens-slapped-with-usd2-billion-grid-upgrade-bill-for-out-of-state-ai-data-centers-state-complains-to-federal-energy-regulators-says-additional-cost-breaks-ratepayer-protection-pledge-promises]. The Energy Dominance Council and 13 PJM-state governors (a politically mixed group) issued a joint statement of principles committing to use state authorities to allocate costs to data centres and protect residential customers [Source: https://www.powerpolicy.net/p/data-centers-could-make-or-break].

### Senate and federal legislative attention

Senate Energy and Natural Resources Committee Chairman Mike Lee held a hearing on 23 July 2025 titled "To Identify Challenges to Meeting Increased Electricity Demand" [Source: https://www.energy.senate.gov/2025/7/chairman-lee-holds-hearing-to-address-urgent-challenges-facing-america-s-skyrocketing-energy-demands; https://bgrdc.com/to-identify-challenges-to-meeting-increased-electricity-demand/]. Ranking Member John Barrasso (R-WY) explicitly named data centres as the biggest source of new demand. Senator Elizabeth Warren and three other Senate Democrats opened a formal investigation into hyperscaler responsibility for utility-cost increases, sending letters to Amazon, Google, Meta and Microsoft [Source: https://www.warren.senate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/senator-warren-lawmakers-open-investigation-into-big-tech-data-centers-role-in-driving-up-families-utility-costs; https://www.geekwire.com/2025/senators-probe-ai-tech-giants-over-electric-bills-amazon-says-its-data-centers-pay-more-than-their-share/; https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/three-senate-democrats-send-hyperscalers-letters-as-they-investigate-data-centers-impact-on-us-electricity-prices/].

### Is opposition left- or right-coded?

Both. The clearest pattern in 2025/26 is that opposition is *not* polarising along usual lines:
- the consumer-protection / rate-shift frame (centred on residential bills) appeals across the spectrum;
- the environmental-justice / climate frame is left-coded;
- the property-rights / farmland / NIMBY frame is right-coded;
- the fiscal-conservatism / tax-break frame is right-coded.

Where opposition has prevailed politically — Powhatan VA, Warrenton VA, Saline Township MI, Prince William County VA — it has typically combined two or more of these frames into local coalitions of unusual makeup.

## 4. Federal and state regulatory shifts

### FERC: from rejection to reform

The most important single regulatory event in this story was FERC's 1 November 2024 order rejecting PJM's amended Interconnection Service Agreement that would have raised the Talen-Susquehanna nuclear-Amazon co-located load from 300 MW to 480 MW. The 2-1 vote (Christie and See in favour of rejection, Phillips dissenting) found that PJM had failed to meet its burden of justifying deviations from the pro-forma ISA, and that the proposed structure would have allowed Amazon to take services from the PJM transmission system without paying for them — improperly shifting costs to other ratepayers. The original 300 MW co-located load was preserved [Source: https://www.utilitydive.com/news/ferc-interconnection-isa-talen-amazon-data-center-susquehanna-exelon/731841/; https://www.powermag.com/ferc-blocks-pjm-proposal-to-expand-amazon-data-center-load-at-susquehanna-nuclear-plant/; https://www.ans.org/news/article-6534/ferc-rejects-interconnection-deal-for-talenamazon-data-centers/].

On 22 November 2024 Constellation Energy filed a complaint at FERC alleging that the PJM tariff was unjust because it lacked clear rules for "Fully Isolated Co-Located Load". On 20 February 2025 FERC voted unanimously to launch a Section 206 show-cause proceeding combining the Constellation complaint with the prior November 2024 technical conference [Source: https://ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-orders-action-co-location-issues-related-data-centers-running-ai; https://www.stoel.com/insights/publications/ferc-institutes-show-cause-proceeding-to-address-co-location-issues-related-to-data-centers-running-ai-in-pjm-and-requests-industry-comments].

On 18 December 2025 FERC issued its order disposing of the show-cause proceeding. The Commission found the PJM tariff unjust and unreasonable for failing to address co-location with sufficient clarity, and directed PJM to create three new transmission services for co-located load willing to limit grid withdrawals: Interim Non-Firm Transmission Service, Firm Contract Demand and Non-Firm Contract Demand. PJM was ordered to file tariff revisions by 20 January 2026. A paper hearing was opened to set just-and-reasonable rates for the new services [Source: https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-directs-nations-largest-grid-operator-create-new-rules-embrace-innovation-and; https://www.bakerbotts.com/thought-leadership/publications/2025/december/ferc-issues-order-providing-guidance-for-co-locating-power-plants-with-data-centers-within-pjm; https://www.utilitydive.com/news/ferc-pjm-colocation-data-center/808368/; https://www.daypitney.com/alert-federal-energy-regulatory-commission-orders-major-pjm-tariff-reforms-for-co-located-load-and-behind-the-meter-generation; https://www.morganlewis.com/blogs/datacenterbytes/2025/12/ferc-weighs-in-on-pjm-data-center-co-location-rules].

The substantive effect of the December 2025 order is to require co-located large loads either to take fully grid-integrated transmission service (and pay for it) or to accept enforceable contractual limits on grid withdrawals. It is an attempt to thread a needle: keep AI investment economically viable while preventing large loads from free-riding on transmission paid for by other ratepayers.

### State PSC dissents and orders

The Dominion biennial review, AEP Ohio data centre tariff, Entergy/Meta dissent and PPL settlement covered above are all unfavourable to incumbent data-centre cost-allocation practice. The aggregate signal across PSCs in late 2025 and early 2026 is that data centres are being moved into separate rate classes with minimum-bill provisions and stranded-cost protection.

## 7. Are attitudes changing?

Yes. The evidence is consistent across three independent measures:

1. **Polling**: the Virginia 35-per-cent comfort number (down from 69 per cent in two years) is the most striking single data point. National Pew data, Morning Consult and the University of Chicago survey all show clear majorities (a) more concerned than excited about AI overall and (b) negative on the environmental and energy footprint of data centres specifically. Concern is rising in absolute terms and the energy-bill prime increases anti-data-centre sentiment by six points in the only experiment we have.

2. **Political behaviour**: Data Center Watch's $98 billion of blocked or delayed projects by mid-2025; the Warrenton, Virginia council being voted out and replaced by a council unanimously opposed to Amazon's local data centre; Virginia's flagship cost-shift bills passing the General Assembly twice in 2026 (forcing the Governor's amendments and the General Assembly's rejection of those amendments); state legislatures passing more than 40 substantive bills in 21 states in 2025; the AEP Ohio tariff and PPL settlement creating new precedents for separate large-load rate classes.

3. **Regulatory behaviour**: FERC's November 2024 rejection of the Talen-Amazon ISA, its February 2025 show-cause order, and its December 2025 order requiring PJM to create new transmission services together represent the most consequential federal regulatory pivot on large-load arrangements in a generation. State PSCs (Virginia SCC, Louisiana PSC dissent, Ohio PUCO, Pennsylvania PUC) are all moving in the same direction.

The opposition is broadening from local to generalised. The political coalition is genuinely cross-cutting: GOP-led states (Texas, Ohio, Georgia) are at least as activist on rate allocation as Democratic-led states (Virginia, Maryland, New Jersey). The Trump administration is pulling in the opposite direction with permit acceleration, the Stargate project, and federal-land siting; this creates a federal-vs-states tension that will likely sharpen through the 2026 midterms, especially in Pennsylvania where GOP incumbents face the most acute electoral exposure.

The case against treating this as a settled trend is twofold: opposition has been building during a period of concentrated rate shocks (the PJM 2025/26 and 2026/27 auctions), and supportive polling on jobs and tax revenue still exists. If capacity prices stabilise or fall — a possibility if announced gas builds materialise on time — public salience may decline. But the structural shift in PSC rate design, codified in tariff orders that will operate for decades, will outlast the news cycle.

## Caveats

- **Attribution**: Data centre demand is one driver of rate increases alongside coal retirements, gas-price volatility, transmission build-outs and broader electrification. Monitoring Analytics and IEEFA both publish methodology; their numbers attribute a marginal share to data centres, not all of the increase.
- **Polling sample frames**: where polls were commissioned by an advocacy group (CCAN Action Fund / Global Strategy Group) the source has been flagged. Pew, Wason Center, Washington Post-Schar School and Pew Research are non-advocacy.
- **Gaps**: I did not locate a Texas-specific published data-centre poll number; I did not locate evidence of an active EPA Title VI complaint against a data centre as of April 2026 (the Memphis xAI case is being litigated under the Clean Air Act citizen-suit provisions, not Title VI); I did not find detailed academic work specifically on data centre siting equity. The brief mentioned "SB 2627" in Texas, which does not appear in standard legal summaries — it may be a misnumbering, with SB 6 being the operative bill.
- **Forward indicators**: PSC orders and FERC tariff revisions take effect over years. Numerical residential bill impacts cited above are estimates from IEEFA, NRDC and consumer-advocate organisations and are subject to revision as tariffs are finalised.
- **Industry sources**: Data Center Coalition, EPSA, Data Center Frontier, and similar industry/trade bodies have been used for facts (lobbying spend, technical detail) but not for normative claims.
